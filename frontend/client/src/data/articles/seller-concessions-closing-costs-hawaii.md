# What Hawaii Buyers Don't Know About Closing Costs — And How to Get the Seller to Pay Them

*Last Updated: June 2026*

**In May 2026, 46% of home sellers nationally gave concessions to buyers — the highest rate in years. Yet in Hawaii, where closing costs are the second-biggest barrier to homeownership, most buyers are still leaving the table without asking the seller to cover them. If you know how to structure the ask, especially with a VA loan, you can effectively walk into a Hawaii home with zero out-of-pocket costs.**

Most Hawaii homebuyers obsess over two numbers: the purchase price and the down payment. They spend months saving $20,000 to $40,000 for a down payment, finally get under contract, and then get hit with a reality check three days before closing. 

That reality check is the Closing Disclosure, and it shows another $12,000 to $20,000 in closing costs that they somehow have to come up with in cash.

In Hawaii, closing costs are the silent killer of real estate transactions. But right now, the market is shifting. On Oahu, condo inventory is rising, and the market is moving decisively into buyer's territory. Nationally, nearly half of all sellers are offering concessions to get deals done. 

The move for Hawaii buyers right now isn't just negotiating the price — it's negotiating the cash to close. Here is exactly how seller concessions work, what the limits are for each loan type, and the strategy to get the seller to pay your closing costs.

---

## The Iceberg: What You Are Actually Paying For

For a full breakdown of what closing costs include, see our guide to [understanding closing costs in Hawaii](/knowledge-base/understanding-closing-costs-hawaii). But generally, when you buy a home in Hawaii, your closing costs will run between 2% and 4% of the purchase price. On an $800,000 property, that is $16,000 to $32,000 in cash you need *on top* of your down payment.

These costs are split into two categories:
1. **Hard Costs:** Lender fees, appraisal, title insurance, and escrow fees.
2. **Prepaids:** Prorated property taxes, the first year of homeowner's insurance (and hurricane insurance, if applicable), and prepaid mortgage interest.

Hawaii has some unique advantages and disadvantages here. Our property taxes are the lowest in the nation as a percentage of home value (around 0.27% to 0.40% for owner-occupants in Honolulu), which keeps your prepaid tax burden lower than on the mainland. Furthermore, Hawaii has no state transfer tax for buyers — the conveyance tax is paid entirely by the seller.

However, Hawaii's high purchase prices mean that percentage-based fees (like title insurance and loan origination) translate into massive dollar amounts. And if you are buying a condo, you might have to prepay several months of high HOA fees.

This is where seller concessions come in.

---

## What Are Seller Concessions?

A seller concession (or seller credit) is when the seller agrees to pay a portion of your closing costs from their proceeds at closing. 

It does not change the purchase price of the home. If you buy a house for $800,000 with a $15,000 seller credit, the house still costs $800,000. Your loan amount is still based on $800,000. But at the closing table, the seller gives up $15,000 of their profit, and that money is credited directly to your closing cost bill.

In a competitive seller's market, asking for concessions will get your offer thrown in the trash. But in a shifting market — like the Oahu condo market in summer 2026 — sellers are increasingly willing to offer credits rather than drop their list price. Why? Because a $15,000 credit costs the seller the exact same amount as a $15,000 price reduction, but it maintains the "comps" (comparable sales) in the neighborhood.

For the buyer, the difference is massive. A $15,000 price reduction saves you about $90 a month on your mortgage. A $15,000 seller credit keeps $15,000 of cash in your bank account today.

## The Limits: How Much Can You Ask For?

You cannot ask the seller to give you cash back at closing, and you cannot ask them to pay your down payment. Seller concessions can only be used to cover actual closing costs, prepaids, and discount points (rate buydowns). 

Every loan program has a strict cap on how much the seller can contribute.

### VA Loans: The Ultimate Concession Strategy

For military buyers in Hawaii, the VA loan rules regarding seller concessions are arguably the most powerful and least understood benefit of the program.

Under VA guidelines, the seller can pay **ALL** of the buyer's customary closing costs (appraisal, title, escrow, recording fees, credit report) with **no limit**. 

*On top of that*, the seller can contribute up to **4% of the purchase price** in additional "seller concessions." These additional concessions can be used to pay for:
- The VA Funding Fee
- Prepaid property taxes and insurance
- Discount points to permanently buy down the interest rate
- Payment of the buyer's collections or judgments to qualify for the loan

**The Move:** If you are a military buyer purchasing an $800,000 home with a VA loan, the seller could theoretically pay $15,000 in standard closing costs, PLUS up to $32,000 (4%) in concessions to cover your VA funding fee and prepay your taxes. If negotiated correctly, a Hawaii military buyer can effectively walk into a home with zero out-of-pocket costs.

### FHA Loans: The 6% Cap

FHA loans are highly generous with seller concessions, allowing the seller to contribute up to **6% of the purchase price** toward the buyer's closing costs, prepaids, and discount points. 

On a $600,000 Oahu condo, that is a maximum credit of $36,000. Because closing costs rarely reach 6% of the purchase price, FHA buyers often use the excess credit to buy down their interest rate.

### Conventional Loans: Tiered by Down Payment

Conventional loans tie the maximum seller concession to your down payment:
- **Less than 10% down:** Maximum 3% of purchase price
- **10% to 24.9% down:** Maximum 6% of purchase price
- **25% or more down:** Maximum 9% of purchase price

For a first-time buyer putting 5% down on a $700,000 home, the maximum seller credit is 3%, or $21,000. This is usually just enough to cover standard closing costs and prepaids in Hawaii.

## How to Structure the Ask in Today's Market

You don't just blindly ask for $20,000. You have to structure the offer so the seller sees the net math.

If a home is listed at $800,000 and has been sitting on the market for 45 days, the seller is likely considering a price drop. Instead of offering $780,000, you offer the full $800,000 but ask for a $20,000 seller credit toward closing costs.

To the seller, the "net" is exactly the same: they walk away with $780,000 before their own fees. 

To you, the buyer, the difference is life-changing. If you offered $780,000, you would still have to drain your savings to pay $20,000 in closing costs. By offering $800,000 with a $20,000 credit, you finance that $20,000 into your 30-year mortgage. Your monthly payment goes up slightly, but you keep your $20,000 cash reserve intact for emergencies, furniture, or renovations.

### The Appraisal Caveat

There is one catch: the home must appraise for the full purchase price. If you offer $800,000 with a $20,000 credit, but the home only appraises for $780,000, your lender will base the loan on the $780,000 value. You would then have to renegotiate the price and the credit with the seller.

## The Bottom Line

In a shifting market, cash is king. Do not drain your life savings to pay title fees and prepaid taxes if the seller is willing to pay them for you. Work with a lender who understands the exact concession limits for your loan type, and have your real estate agent write the credit into the purchase contract.

### Frequently Asked Questions

**Can seller concessions be used for the down payment?**
No. Seller concessions can only be used for closing costs, prepaid items (like taxes and insurance), discount points, and the VA funding fee. You must still provide your own down payment, unless you are using a 0% down VA or USDA loan.

**What happens if the seller credit is more than my closing costs?**
You cannot receive cash back at closing. If your closing costs are $15,000 and the seller gave you a $20,000 credit, the remaining $5,000 must be used to buy down your interest rate (discount points) or the credit must be reduced to $15,000.

**Does the seller pay the buyer's agent commission in Hawaii?**
Following the 2024 NAR settlement, buyer's agent compensation is fully negotiable. Seller concessions can be used to pay your buyer's agent fee if that is written into the purchase contract and allowed by your specific loan program.

**Do seller concessions affect the appraisal?**
Appraisers note seller concessions in their report because they affect the net sale price, but the property must still justify the gross purchase price based on comparable sales in the neighborhood.
